Apex Systems Performance Review Process
Effective January 1, 2026

This document describes the performance review process at Apex Systems, including the review cycle, self-evaluation and peer review procedures, manager assessment, calibration, the rating scale, compensation linkage, promotion criteria, and ongoing check-in practices. The performance review process is designed to give every employee clear, honest, and actionable feedback, to recognize strong contributors, to support employees who need development, and to ensure that compensation and promotion decisions are fair and grounded in evidence.

Review Cycle

Apex Systems operates a two-cycle review process: a mid-year review in June and an annual review in December. The two cycles serve different purposes and have different scopes.

Mid-Year Review (June): The mid-year review is a lightweight check-in focused on progress against goals set at the start of the year, early identification of performance concerns, and course corrections before the end of the year. The mid-year review does not result in a formal performance rating or compensation change. It produces a written summary of the manager's assessment of the employee's progress, any updated or revised goals for the second half of the year, and a record of the conversation in the HRIS. All employees must complete a mid-year self-reflection and receive a written mid-year summary from their manager.

Annual Review (December): The annual review is the primary performance evaluation. It includes a self-evaluation, peer feedback, and a manager assessment. It produces a formal performance rating, which feeds directly into the compensation review cycle and promotion decisions. The annual review timeline runs from late October through mid-December, with ratings finalized before December 15 and compensation adjustments effective January 1 of the following year.

Review Timeline

Annual review timeline:
Late October: People Operations opens the review cycle in the HRIS. Employees and managers are notified to begin self-evaluations and peer nomination.
November 1 through November 15: Self-evaluations and peer nominations due.
November 1 through November 22: Peer reviews submitted.
November 15 through November 30: Manager drafts assessments.
December 1 through December 8: Calibration sessions held by department.
December 8 through December 12: Managers finalize ratings and assessments following calibration.
December 13 through December 15: Ratings approved by department heads and People Operations.
December 16 through December 20: Managers deliver reviews to employees. Compensation letters distributed.
January 1: Compensation adjustments effective.

Self-Evaluation

Every employee completes a self-evaluation as the first step in the annual review process. The self-evaluation is submitted through the HRIS and is visible to the employee's manager but not to peers. The self-evaluation should be honest, specific, and evidence-based. It is an opportunity for the employee to document accomplishments that might not be fully visible to the manager, to reflect candidly on areas for development, and to provide context for any gaps or challenges during the year.

Self-evaluation prompts:

Goals and accomplishments: List the goals you set at the start of the year or during the mid-year review. For each goal, describe your level of achievement and provide specific evidence of impact. Where goals were not met, explain the circumstances.

Key contributions: Describe two to four contributions from the past year that you are most proud of. Focus on outcomes and impact, not activities. Quantify impact where possible.

Collaboration and influence: Describe how you worked with colleagues, cross-functional partners, and stakeholders. Provide specific examples of times you contributed to team success beyond your individual scope.

Values alignment: Reflect on how your work this year demonstrated Apex Systems values: intellectual curiosity, ownership, collaboration, transparency, and commitment to quality. Provide one specific example for at least three values.

Development and growth: What did you learn this year? What skills, capabilities, or knowledge did you develop or deepen? What aspects of your work do you want to develop further in the coming year?

Looking ahead: What are your goals for the coming year? What support or resources would help you achieve them? Are there stretch opportunities or new responsibilities you would like to take on?

Self-evaluations should be two to four pages. Shorter submissions may not give the manager sufficient material to write a complete assessment. Overly long submissions that repeat the same examples are less effective than concise, substantive responses.

Peer Review Process

Peer reviews provide managers with perspectives from colleagues who work closely with the employee being reviewed. Peer feedback is intended to surface information about collaboration, reliability, communication, and impact that may not be visible to the manager alone.

Peer Nomination: Each employee nominates three to five peers for review. Nominations are submitted during the first two weeks of the review cycle. The employee's manager reviews the nominations and may add or substitute reviewers to ensure a balanced and relevant set of perspectives. Managers should ensure that peer reviewers include people the employee has worked with directly, both within the team and cross-functionally. Nominating only close friends or allies is not acceptable.

Peer Review Questions: Peers are asked to complete a structured form with the following prompts:
In what context did you work with this person in the past year? Describe specific projects or interactions.
What are this person's greatest strengths? Provide specific examples.
What is one area where this person could have more impact if they developed or changed their approach? Be specific and constructive.
On a five-point scale, how effectively does this person collaborate with others? (1 = Significant concerns; 5 = Role model for the team)
Is there anything else you want the manager to know about this person's performance or contributions?

Peers are encouraged to be specific and candid. Vague positive feedback ("great to work with, always helpful") is less useful than specific examples. Peer reviews are confidential: the manager reads the peer feedback but the employee does not see individual peer responses. The manager synthesizes peer themes and includes them anonymously in the manager assessment.

Confidentiality does not mean immunity from accountability. If a peer review contains information suggesting misconduct or policy violations, People Operations may follow up.

Manager Assessment

The manager writes the formal performance assessment after reviewing the employee's self-evaluation and peer feedback. The assessment is the primary document that defines the employee's performance for the year. It must be evidence-based, specific, and balanced.

Manager assessment sections:

Overall performance summary: A two to three paragraph narrative summary of the employee's performance during the year. This section should synthesize the most significant contributions, the quality of the employee's work, their growth relative to prior performance, and any notable gaps.

Goal achievement: For each goal set during the year, the manager assesses the degree to which the goal was achieved and the quality of the work. This section should reference specific outcomes and, where appropriate, note circumstances that affected performance.

Strengths: Two to four specific, evidence-based strengths observed during the year. Each strength should be supported by at least one concrete example.

Development areas: One to two areas for improvement with specific, actionable recommendations. Development areas should be framed constructively and focused on what the employee can do differently, not on personality or fixed traits.

Peer feedback themes: A summary of themes from peer reviews, presented anonymously. The manager identifies patterns across peer responses — both positive themes and development themes — and includes them in the assessment.

Values alignment: An assessment of how the employee's behavior and work demonstrated Apex Systems values. This section may highlight strong alignment or note specific situations where values were not upheld.

Rating: The manager proposes a rating following the rating scale described below. The proposed rating is subject to calibration before it is finalized.

Rating Scale

All employees receive one of five ratings in the annual review:

Exceptional: Reserved for employees who significantly exceeded all expectations and made outsized contributions to team and company outcomes. Exceptional performance is rare: employees at this level are operating well above the expectations for their role and level, have demonstrated a clear pattern of impact, and serve as a model for others. Fewer than ten percent of employees in any team or department should receive this rating in any given year.

Exceeds Expectations: The employee exceeded expectations in most areas and met expectations in all others. Contributions were consistently strong, and the employee demonstrated meaningful impact beyond their core responsibilities. This is a strong performance rating that reflects excellent work.

Meets Expectations: The employee met expectations for their role and level. Contributions were solid, goals were substantially achieved, and the employee operated effectively as a member of the team. This is the most common rating and reflects good, reliable performance. Employees who consistently meet expectations are performing well.

Developing: The employee met expectations in some areas but fell short in others. There are one or more significant gaps in performance, skill, or values alignment that must be addressed. A Developing rating triggers a formal development plan for the following year. Employees with a Developing rating are not eligible for a merit increase and are not eligible for promotion in the subsequent cycle.

Not Meeting Expectations: The employee did not meet expectations in multiple significant areas. This rating is reserved for cases of sustained underperformance or significant misconduct. A Not Meeting Expectations rating results in a mandatory Performance Improvement Plan. Continued failure to improve following a PIP results in termination. Employees receiving this rating must be discussed with People Operations before the rating is finalized.

Calibration Sessions

Calibration sessions are held after manager assessments are drafted but before ratings are finalized. The purpose of calibration is to ensure consistency of ratings across the organization, to surface bias, and to ensure that the distribution of ratings reflects actual performance rather than managerial generosity or severity.

Calibration is facilitated by People Operations and attended by all managers within a department and the department head. Each manager presents their proposed ratings for their direct reports, summarizes the key evidence supporting each rating, and takes questions from other managers and the department head. The group discusses cases where ratings seem high or low relative to the evidence presented, where ratings for employees at the same level appear inconsistent, or where a manager's rating distribution appears skewed compared to other managers.

Managers may change proposed ratings following calibration. Final ratings must be approved by the department head and People Operations. Employees are not present during calibration. Managers must not share the content of calibration discussions with employees.

Calibration is particularly important for borderline cases — employees proposed for Exceptional or Not Meeting Expectations ratings, employees proposed for promotion, and employees at the same level where there are large rating disparities. Managers who disagree with a calibration outcome may escalate to the Chief People Officer, whose decision is final.

Performance Improvement Plans

A Performance Improvement Plan is required for any employee receiving a Developing or Not Meeting Expectations rating. The PIP is a formal document that defines specific performance gaps, sets measurable improvement goals, establishes a timeline for improvement, and describes the consequences of continued underperformance.

PIP development: The manager drafts the PIP in consultation with People Operations. The PIP must include: a description of the specific performance gaps with examples, the performance standard that is expected, the specific and measurable goals the employee must achieve, the timeline (typically thirty to ninety days), the support and resources the company will provide, and the consequence of failing to meet the PIP goals.

PIP execution: The manager meets with the employee to deliver the PIP and discuss its contents. The employee signs the PIP to acknowledge receipt; signing does not indicate agreement. The manager holds weekly one-on-ones with the employee during the PIP period. Progress is documented in writing after each meeting and stored in the HRIS.

PIP outcomes: At the end of the PIP period, the manager assesses whether the employee has met the defined goals. If the goals are met, the PIP is closed with a documented conclusion and the employee continues employment, typically with a Developing rating at the next review cycle. If the goals are not met, the manager consults People Operations and the department head to determine next steps, which may include an extended PIP or termination.

Promotion Criteria

Promotions at Apex Systems are evaluated during the annual review cycle. Promotion recommendations are submitted by managers during calibration and approved by the department head and Chief People Officer.

To be considered for promotion, an employee must:
Have been in their current role for at least twelve months.
Have received a rating of Exceeds Expectations or Exceptional in their most recent annual review.
Have demonstrated sustained performance at the next level, not merely strong performance at the current level. The distinction is critical: promotion recognizes that an employee is already operating at the higher level, not that they have potential to do so in the future.
Have no active PIP or disciplinary action.

Promotion decisions are based on role-specific level criteria maintained by People Operations. Each level in each job family has a defined set of scope, complexity, autonomy, and impact expectations. Managers making a promotion recommendation must write a promotion case that addresses each criterion at the target level with specific evidence from the employee's work.

Compensation Link to Ratings

Annual merit increases are tied to performance ratings. The merit increase budget is set by the CFO and approved by the Board each year. People Operations communicates the merit budget and corresponding increase ranges to managers during the compensation review cycle following calibration.

Merit increase ranges by rating (illustrative; actual ranges are communicated annually based on budget):
Exceptional: 8 to 12 percent merit increase, plus eligibility for discretionary bonus above target.
Exceeds Expectations: 5 to 8 percent merit increase, plus discretionary bonus at or above target.
Meets Expectations: 2.5 to 5 percent merit increase, plus discretionary bonus at target.
Developing: 0 percent merit increase. No bonus eligibility for the year.
Not Meeting Expectations: 0 percent merit increase. No bonus eligibility. PIP required.

Increases are calculated on base salary and are effective January 1. Total compensation for each employee, including base, bonus, and equity, is reviewed against market data and internal equity during the compensation review cycle. Adjustments for market or internal equity may be made independent of the merit budget in cases where significant gaps are identified.

30-60-90 Day Check-Ins

In addition to the formal review cycle, managers conduct structured thirty-day, sixty-day, and ninety-day check-ins with new hires and employees who have changed roles. These check-ins are documented in the HRIS and focus on onboarding progress, early goal alignment, and identification of any early challenges.

Thirty-day check-in: Focus on orientation completeness, understanding of role and team, initial observations, and any blockers the employee has encountered. The manager should confirm that the employee has access to all systems and resources needed, has met key stakeholders, and has a clear picture of near-term expectations.

Sixty-day check-in: Focus on early contribution, progress on initial goals, quality of cross-functional relationships, and development of role-specific skills. The manager should assess whether the employee is ramping at the expected pace and provide specific feedback on early work.

Ninety-day check-in: Focus on independent contribution, alignment on annual goals, and identification of any development needs or growth opportunities. The ninety-day check-in is the final structured onboarding milestone. By this point, the employee should be operating with minimal supervision within their defined scope.

Employees who are not on track at the ninety-day mark should be placed on an informal development plan to address specific gaps before the six-month mark. If performance concerns persist at six months, a formal PIP may be initiated.

Manager Responsibilities

Managers are responsible for the quality and fairness of the performance process for their direct reports. Specific responsibilities include: providing ongoing, real-time feedback throughout the year rather than saving all feedback for the annual review; conducting the mid-year and annual reviews on schedule; writing specific, evidence-based assessments; avoiding bias in ratings, including recency bias, similarity bias, and leniency or severity bias; delivering difficult feedback directly and constructively; and following through on development commitments made during review conversations.

People Operations conducts manager calibration training each year before the review cycle opens. Participation is required for all people managers. Managers who consistently receive peer or employee feedback indicating unfair or unclear performance management practices are subject to coaching or additional accountability measures.

Employees who believe their review was inaccurate, biased, or conducted improperly may raise a concern with People Operations. People Operations will review the manager's documentation and calibration record and determine whether any corrective action is warranted.
