Apex Systems Budget Policy
Fiscal Year 2026 — Effective January 1, 2026

This policy defines how annual and quarterly budgets are constructed, approved, managed, and monitored at Apex Systems. It applies to every cost center owner, functional leader, finance business partner, and any employee with purchase authority. Understanding this policy is a prerequisite for any employee who manages a budget line, approves vendor contracts, or initiates headcount requests.

Budget Cycle and Calendar

The fiscal year at Apex runs from January 1 through December 31. Budget planning follows a structured annual calendar designed to give functional leaders enough time to build thoughtful plans while giving finance enough time to consolidate, stress-test, and present a credible plan to the board.

September 1: The CFO issues top-down guidance to each functional leader. The guidance specifies the target revenue growth rate for the year, the total headcount budget by function, and the maximum operating expense envelope. It is not a blank check for functional leaders to fill — it is a constraint to plan within.

September 1 to October 1: Functional leaders conduct internal planning sessions. Each leader works with their finance business partner to identify the investments required to meet the revenue and efficiency targets. Decisions made during this window should be grounded in concrete business cases, not aspirational wish lists.

October 15: Functional leaders submit their bottoms-up plans to the finance business partner. Plans are submitted in the standardized budget template, which includes: personnel cost by level and role, software and tools cost by vendor, professional services, facilities and travel, and a narrative memo explaining how the investments connect to company objectives.

October 15 to November 15: Finance consolidates the submissions, identifies overlaps and gaps, runs sensitivity analyses, and produces the integrated company plan. Finance schedules individual working sessions with each functional leader to reconcile where the bottoms-up plan exceeds the top-down envelope.

November 15 to December 5: The senior leadership team reviews the integrated plan in two sessions, one focused on revenue and go-to-market, one focused on operating expense and headcount. Material trade-offs are surfaced for CEO-level decision.

December board meeting: The board of directors reviews and approves the final annual plan, including the total headcount target, the revenue plan, the operating expense budget, and the capital expenditure budget.

December 20: Functional leaders receive their approved cost center budgets and are expected to communicate the plan to their teams before January 1.

Mid-Year Reforecasts

In addition to the annual plan, Apex produces two formal reforecasts per year.

Q2 reforecast (June): Based on actual results through May and a revised assessment of second-half performance, the Q2 reforecast updates the full-year outlook for revenue, operating expense, headcount additions, and ending cash balance. The Q2 reforecast may result in approved budget adjustments for functions that are tracking significantly ahead of or behind plan.

Q3 reforecast (September): The Q3 reforecast is the last opportunity to revise the current-year outlook before the annual planning cycle begins again. It also serves as a first look at the following year, providing early signals about where investment levels should increase or decrease.

Both reforecast submissions are due from functional leaders within 10 business days of the reforecast kickoff date communicated by Finance. Submissions that are late or incomplete are escalated to the CFO.

Material variances — greater than 5% on any revenue line item, greater than $250,000 on any expense line item, or greater than 10 FTEs on the headcount plan — require a written variance explanation in the reforecast memo. Explanations should describe the cause of the variance, the impact on the business, and the corrective action if the variance is unfavorable.

Quarterly Budget Review Schedule

Apex Systems conducts four formal quarterly budget reviews each year. These reviews are distinct from the monthly finance leadership review and the twice-yearly reforecast process. The quarterly reviews are the primary forum where department heads and the CFO assess performance against plan, align on a revised full-year outlook, and make formal decisions about budget adjustments. Each review follows a fixed calendar, a defined attendee list, a structured agenda, and a documented approval process.

Q1 Budget Review — April

The Q1 review is held during the first two weeks of April, covering actual results for January through March. The review is a half-day session, typically three hours, scheduled by the FP&A team no later than March 25.

Attendees: CFO, VP Finance, FP&A lead, all finance business partners, and all department heads. The CEO attends the opening segment covering company-level revenue performance and exits before the cost center breakout sessions. Legal and People Operations department heads attend only their respective cost center segment.

What is reviewed: Actuals versus budget for Q1 across all cost centers and natural account categories. Variance analysis for every line item exceeding the materiality threshold of 10% or $25,000, whichever is greater. Personnel cost actuals versus headcount plan, including any open requisitions, recent hires, and attrition. Software and SaaS spend versus contracted commitments. A Q1 cash flow summary and comparison to the cash forecast approved in December. Early signals on full-year revenue tracking. The FP&A team presents a preliminary Q1 reforecast for the full year based on Q1 actuals and any known changes to business conditions.

Approval process: Within five business days of the Q1 review, department heads submit any budget amendment requests identified during the session to their finance business partner. The FP&A team consolidates requests and presents a recommended amendment package to the CFO within ten business days. The CFO approves, rejects, or defers each amendment. Approved amendments are reflected in the budget system by April 30 and communicated to cost center owners in writing.

Q2 Budget Review — July

The Q2 review is held during the first two weeks of July, covering actual results for April through June and the full first half of the year. This is the most consequential quarterly review because it informs the Q2 reforecast, which may result in material adjustments to the second-half plan. The review is a full-day session, typically five to six hours.

Attendees: CFO, CEO, VP Finance, FP&A lead, all finance business partners, all department heads, and the Chief Revenue Officer. The board audit committee chair receives the Q2 review package as a read-only distribution within five business days of the session.

What is reviewed: Actuals versus budget for Q2 and the full first half. Detailed variance analysis with written explanations required for all variances exceeding the materiality threshold. Updated full-year revenue forecast compared to the annual plan, including deal pipeline coverage, renewal rates, and expansion revenue. Second-half operating expense plan versus first-half run rate. Headcount plan versus actuals, including a review of open requisitions and projected hiring velocity for the remainder of the year. Cash position and updated twelve-month cash flow forecast. A formal Q2 reforecast for the full year, presented by FP&A, which the CFO and CEO must approve before it is distributed externally or used in board materials.

The Q2 review session concludes with a structured decision segment where the CFO and CEO make explicit go or no-go decisions on any material investments, headcount additions, or spend holds that were flagged during the session. Decisions are documented in the Q2 review minutes, which are distributed to all attendees and retained in the Finance document archive.

Approval process: The Q2 reforecast, once approved by the CFO and CEO in the session or within three business days afterward, constitutes the revised operating plan for the second half. Department heads whose second-half budgets differ materially from the original annual plan are issued amended cost center budgets by July 31. Any second-half headcount additions approved in the Q2 review are treated as plan-approved and do not require a separate CFO approval when the job description is posted.

Q3 Budget Review — October

The Q3 review is held during the first two weeks of October, covering actual results for July through September. Because October also marks the start of the annual planning cycle for the following year, the Q3 review serves two purposes: it closes out the current-year outlook and provides the baseline assumptions for the following year's budget guidance.

Attendees: CFO, VP Finance, FP&A lead, all finance business partners, and all department heads. The CEO attends a summary segment at the end of the session focused on year-end outlook and next-year preliminary signals.

What is reviewed: Actuals versus budget for Q3 and year-to-date through September. Variance analysis with required explanations. Updated full-year revenue and expense outlook based on Q3 actuals. A fourth-quarter expense forecast by cost center, showing expected spend for October through December and projected year-end actuals. Assessment of any budget remaining in cost centers that is unlikely to be spent by year-end, which may be subject to reallocation or carry-forward restrictions. A preliminary view of the following year's cost base, including known contract renewals, anticipated headcount needs, and major technology or infrastructure investments under consideration. The FP&A team presents the Q3 reforecast for the full year; this is a working document, not a formally approved reforecast, and is used primarily to set expectations for the December board materials.

Approval process: Within five business days of the Q3 review, department heads confirm their Q4 spending plans with their finance business partner. Any unspent budget that a department head intends to deploy in Q4 must be re-justified if it represents a new commitment not previously approved. Unspent budget that will not be deployed in Q4 is flagged for year-end close. Budget reallocation requests arising from the Q3 review follow the standard budget transfer process and require CFO approval for cross-department transfers exceeding $50,000.

Q4 and Annual Review — January

The Q4 and annual review is held in January, covering actual results for October through December and the full fiscal year. This is the retrospective review of the year just closed and is scheduled after the accounting close is complete, typically the third or fourth week of January.

Attendees: CFO, CEO, VP Finance, FP&A lead, all finance business partners, all department heads, and the full senior leadership team. The board of directors receives the full-year results package at the January board meeting, which follows this review.

What is reviewed: Full-year actuals versus annual budget for every cost center and natural account. Total company revenue versus plan, including analysis by product line, customer segment, and geography where applicable. Full-year headcount actuals versus the plan approved in the prior December board meeting, including total hires, attrition, and ending headcount by function. Operating expense actuals versus plan and versus the Q2 reforecast, to assess forecasting accuracy. Cash position at December 31 versus the forecast. Capital expenditure actuals versus the capitalized asset budget. A written post-mortem from each department head summarizing the three most significant budget variances in their cost center, what drove them, and what changes to planning process or business operations would prevent similar variances in the current year.

The Q4 and annual review also includes a formal assessment of the budget planning process itself. The FP&A team presents a forecast accuracy report showing how each quarterly reforecast compared to actual results, which cost centers had the most chronic variances, and where the planning calendar or template should be adjusted for the current year.

Approval process: No budget amendments are issued following the Q4 and annual review, since the fiscal year is closed. The primary output is the full-year results package presented to the board in January and the lessons-learned documentation incorporated into the planning guidance issued by the CFO on September 1 of the current year. Department heads whose full-year actuals deviated from plan by more than 15% on any single cost category are required to meet with the CFO and their finance business partner in February to discuss root causes and process improvements before the current-year planning cycle begins.

Budget Structure and Coding

Budgets are organized by cost center. Each cost center is a six-digit code assigned by Finance and corresponds to a single functional team with one designated owner and one finance business partner. Cost center owners are responsible for all spending within their center.

Expenses are classified by natural account, which identifies the type of expense:
6100 — Personnel (salaries, bonus, benefits, payroll taxes)
6200 — Software and SaaS subscriptions
6300 — Professional services and consulting
6400 — Facilities and utilities
6500 — Marketing and demand generation
6600 — Travel and entertainment
6700 — Other operating expenses

Every expense transaction committed in the accounting system must carry a cost center code, a natural account code, and, for project-tracked spend, a project code assigned by Finance. Transactions missing required codes are returned to the submitter; vendors are not paid until coding is complete.

Capitalized Versus Operating Expense Classification

Software developed for internal use must be assessed for capitalization eligibility under ASC 350-40. A project is eligible for capitalization when three conditions are met: the project has entered the application development stage (preliminary project activities, including evaluation and selection of alternatives, are complete), the project has a defined scope and estimated budget, and management has authorized and committed resources to completing the project.

Costs eligible for capitalization include external consulting fees for development work, employee salaries for time directly attributable to development, and interest during development. Costs that are not eligible include training, data migration, maintenance, and overhead allocations.

The Finance team maintains a capitalization register for all active projects. Cost center owners must notify Finance when a project begins the application development stage, so that capitalization can begin from the correct date. The capitalization register is reviewed quarterly; projects that are no longer in active development are closed and no further costs are capitalized.

Cloud infrastructure costs, third-party SaaS subscriptions regardless of contract length, and routine maintenance are expensed as incurred. Prepaid software licenses greater than 12 months are recognized on a straight-line amortization schedule.

Headcount Planning and Approval

Headcount is the largest single component of Apex's operating expense, typically representing 65% to 70% of total operating costs. All headcount planning is conducted at the role level — not the person level — to maintain confidentiality of individual compensation during the planning process.

For each planned role, the budget submission must include: the job title and level, the target start date, the fully loaded annual cost (base salary, target bonus at 100% attainment, employer payroll taxes, and benefits burden of 25% of base), the business justification, and the reporting line.

Net new headcount above the approved plan requires CFO approval before a job description is posted or a recruiter is engaged. Replacement hires for positions that are vacant due to attrition, promotion, or transfer within the approved plan do not require additional CFO approval, provided the new hire is at the same or lower level as the departing employee. Backfills for positions at a higher level than the departing employee require Director-level approval and must be reviewed by the finance business partner.

Conversion of contractors to full-time employees requires headcount approval even if total cost does not increase, because the conversion affects the headcount target.

Spend Authority Levels

The following thresholds define the spend authority for each level of the organization. All dollar amounts represent the total committed value of the transaction, inclusive of all contract years, setup fees, and projected overages.

Individual contributors: up to $500 per individual purchase, within an approved budget line. Typical use case: books, small software tools, registration fees.

People managers: up to $5,000. Typical use case: team software subscriptions, equipment, minor professional services.

Directors: up to $25,000. Typical use case: larger software contracts, recruiting agency fees, departmental events.

Vice presidents: up to $100,000. Typical use case: significant vendor contracts, consulting engagements, hardware purchases.

Chief Financial Officer: up to $500,000. Typical use case: multi-year software agreements, large consulting engagements, banking and financing arrangements.

Chief Executive Officer and board of directors: any transaction exceeding $500,000, and any unbudgeted spend exceeding $250,000 at any level.

Approval must be obtained before committing to a purchase, not after. Retroactive approval is not permitted except in emergency situations with CFO authorization.

Vendor Contract Review

Any contract with an annual value exceeding $50,000 must be reviewed by the Legal team before signature. Contracts with an annual value exceeding $200,000 are additionally reviewed by the CFO. Multi-year contracts of any value require a net present value analysis and an early termination fee assessment, both prepared by Finance.

Vendors that will process, store, or transmit customer data or employee personal information must complete the vendor security assessment administered by the Information Security team. Vendors that fail the security assessment may not be contracted until the identified risks are remediated or risk-accepted by the CISO.

The procurement team maintains a vendor registry. Signing a contract with a vendor not in the registry requires Finance to create a new vendor record, which includes beneficial ownership disclosure and payment instructions verified independently from the vendor invoice.

Budget Transfers and Amendments

Within a cost center, transfers between natural accounts of up to $10,000 may be approved by the cost center owner in the accounting system. Transfers between natural accounts exceeding $10,000 require finance business partner approval and a brief justification noting why the original budget coding was incorrect or why business needs have changed.

Transfers between cost centers — regardless of amount — require approval from both cost center owners and the finance business partner for the originating center. Cross-department transfers exceeding $50,000 require CFO approval. Budget transfers are recorded in the budget change log maintained by Finance and are reflected in the monthly reporting.

Monitoring, Reporting, and Variance Management

Finance business partners publish monthly actual-versus-budget reports for each cost center on the fifth business day after the accounting close. The report shows the current-month and year-to-date actuals versus plan, the full-year outlook based on current run rate, and the ending cash position.

Cost center owners are expected to review the report within three business days of publication and submit a variance memo for any line item that is more than 10% or $25,000 off plan, whichever threshold is greater. Variance memos are submitted through the Finance portal and are reviewed by the finance business partner before the monthly finance leadership review.

The monthly finance leadership review, attended by the CFO, VP Finance, FP&A lead, and all finance business partners, summarizes variances across cost centers, identifies emerging risks, and approves any course-correction actions.

Non-Compliance

Expenses submitted outside of policy are returned to the requester for correction before payment is processed. Recurring non-compliance — defined as three or more policy violations by the same employee within a 12-month period — is escalated to the employee's manager and HR Business Partner as a performance matter. Deliberate circumvention of spend authority controls, including splitting transactions to fall below approval thresholds, is treated as a serious conduct violation and may result in termination.
